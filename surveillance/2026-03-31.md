## Papers of Note (outside window but recent)

### Eysenbach — "Can We Really Learn One Representation to Optimize All Rewards?" (Feb 2026)
- **Summary:** Examines whether unsupervised representation learning (e.g., forward-backward representations) can enable optimal control over arbitrary rewards without fine-tuning. Challenges universal-representation assumptions.
- **Relevance:** (b) Semester paper — connects to OT/representation geometry. (a) Pre-NeurIPS — representation learning for multi-agent settings. | **Flag: COMPLEMENTARY**

### Rocktäschel et al. — "DejaQ: Open-Ended Evolution of Diverse, Learnable and Verifiable Problems" (Jan 2026)
- **Summary:** Jointly evolves diverse synthetic math problems alongside model training using LLM-driven mutation, departing from static dataset paradigms.
- **Relevance:** (a) Pre-NeurIPS — open-ended curriculum generation is directly related to env design. (c) MCTS — verifiable problem generation could pair with tree search. | **Flag: COMPLEMENTARY**

### Baker — "Reasoning Models Struggle to Control their CoT" (March 5, 2026)
- **Summary:** Introduces CoT-Control evaluation; finds reasoning models have low chain-of-thought controllability that decreases with more RL training and test-time compute.
- **Relevance:** (c) MCTS — implications for reasoning/planning with search at test time. | **Flag: BACKGROUND**

### Vinitsky et al. — "Reevaluating Policy Gradient Methods for Imperfect-Information Games" (revised March 17)
- **Summary:** Largest exploitability comparison (7,000+ runs) of DRL algorithms for imperfect-info games; finds PPO competitive with complex FP/DO/CFR approaches.
- **Relevance:** (a) Pre-NeurIPS — directly relevant baseline work from advisor. (c) MCTS — imperfect-info game solving is core to search methods. | **Flag: COMPLEMENTARY**
